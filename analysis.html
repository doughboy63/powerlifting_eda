Motivation

I was reading Scale by Geoffrey West and there was a bit in there talking about how strength scales with size. The underlying theory is this: Think of a cube. If you double the length of the edge, the cross-sectional area of the cube increases by a factor of 4 (= 2^2) and the volume of the cube increases by a factor of eight (=2^3). If you think of something like a 2x4 used in construction, its ability to support a load is largely driven by its cross-sectional area. You can think of volume as a proxy for weight (= volume * density). The theory is the relationship between strength and weight is as follows:

\[\text{Strength} = a \cdot \text{Body Weight}^{2/3} \] where \(a\) is some constant. There is an 1956 article in Science by M.H. Lietzke (https://www.science.org/doi/10.1126/science.124.3220.486) which assesses this relationship using world record weight-lifting totals and finds good agreement with the theory. I don’t have access to the full article because it is behind a paywall but from the first page preview, I know the exponent on body weight was found to be 0.6748 which is very close to the expected 0.66 repeating.

I am curious to see if I can recreated this on a larger more modern dataset.

Setup

I found the following dataset on Kaggle:

https://www.kaggle.com/datasets/open-powerlifting/powerlifting-database?resource=download&select=openpowerlifting-2024-01-06-4c732975.csv.

It is very large (a couple of millions of rows) and so I chose to restrict my analysis to male lifters, who used the minimum in equipment aids (“raw” lifts), did all three lift types (squat, bench press and deadlift) and were “tested” for what I assume are performance enhancing drugs. This filtering was done with the script filtering_raw_data.R and then imported here. I also decided to restrict my analysis to lifters who competed from 2000 and onwards.

Have lifters become stronger?

Like with the Lietzke article, I will use the total weight lifted since this will help to eliminate the effect of lifters who are better at some lifts over others.

Modelling

Given what I learned in the preliminary EDA section, I think I am in a good position to build a model. I’ll do a linear regression with log10(total weight lifted) as the response variable and log10(body weight in kg) as the explanatory variable. The slope between these two variables equals the exponent term of the model presented in the Motivation section. I will also include linear terms in age and year as well as a quadratic term in age in an effort to try and control for these variables.

model1_data <- lifters %>% 
  select(
    total_kg, 
    bodyweight_kg,
    age,
    year
  ) %>% 
  mutate(log_total_kg = log10(total_kg)) %>% 
  mutate(log_bodyweight_kg = log10(bodyweight_kg)) %>%
  mutate(age_squared = age * age) 
    
tidy(lm(log_total_kg ~ log_bodyweight_kg + age_squared + age + year, model1_data))
## # A tibble: 5 × 5
##   term               estimate   std.error statistic  p.value
##   <chr>                 <dbl>       <dbl>     <dbl>    <dbl>
## 1 (Intercept)       -1.42     0.0753          -18.9 1.04e-79
## 2 log_bodyweight_kg  0.548    0.00158         347.  0       
## 3 age_squared       -0.000108 0.000000766    -141.  0       
## 4 age                0.00645  0.0000612       105.  0       
## 5 year               0.00149  0.0000373        40.0 0

Taking an alpha of 5%, we see that all terms in the model are statistically significant. The slope associated with the log10(bodyweight) is 0.548 (0.001). This is less than the theoretical 0.66 repeating and the 0.675 measured by Lietzke in 1956. Maybe my controls are working that well. To test this, I’ll fit another model where I will control for year and age directly through filtering. I will only consider lifters between the ages of 20 and 30 and I will only use competitions from 2023 (n = 27020).

model2_data <- model1_data %>% 
  filter(between(age, 20, 30)) %>%
  filter(year == 2023)

tidy(lm(log_total_kg ~ log_bodyweight_kg, model2_data))
## # A tibble: 2 × 5
##   term              estimate std.error statistic p.value
##   <chr>                <dbl>     <dbl>     <dbl>   <dbl>
## 1 (Intercept)          1.76    0.00988      178.       0
## 2 log_bodyweight_kg    0.515   0.00508      101.       0

I find a value of 0.515 (0.005) for the exponent which is even further from my original estimate. It follows that I will stay with my original estimate of 0.548 (0.001) for the exponent value.
